Overview of a Single Spin and Single Quantum Dot#

The Loss-DiVincenzo (LD) spin qubit is a single-electron spin qubit that consists of a direct mapping between spin operators and encoded Pauli operators. These qubits involve electrons tunneling to/from fermionic electron baths. Higher energy quantum dot spin states will allow for tunneling to the Fermi sea, and the static magnetic field of the electron bath will prevent tunneling from lower-energy spin states. The spin state of the electron, and thus the qubit state, is determined by the presence or absence of tunneling events. The Loss-DiVincenzo model serves as a basis for further developments in spin-qubits. (Based on Burkard et al ).

To further elaborate on the quantum dot itself, it is a structure filled with electrons that is coupled to electron reservoirs. As seen in both Figure 1 of Hanson and in the animation, electrons from the reservoirs from either side of dot tunnel in and out based on the Fermi energy levels. Single quantum dots involve the loading/unloading of single electrons (or coupled, discussed later) in and out from the reservoirs, a source and a drain where the flow of the electrons move from source to dot to drain, with tunnel barriers as the connection between.

Coulomb Blockades#

The number of electrons traveling within a quantum dot is controlled via Coulomb blockade. Since the dot is extremely small, when an electron is inside a quantum dot, there is a strong coulomb repulsion that prevents further electrons from tunneling into the dot. The further electrons would have to an energy that excedes the Coulomb energy

\[E_c = \frac{e^2}{2C}\]

where \(e\) is the charge of the electron and \(C\) is the total capacitance of the dot. As the dot gets smaller, we can note that the overall capacitance of the dot decreases, thus \(E_c\) increases. Thus, adding an additional electron would cost a lot of energy. We can note that this Coulomb blockade is also dictated by the temperature. The thermal energy is \(k_BT\) where \(T\) is the temperature and \(k_B\) is the Boltzmann constant. Thus, at high temperatures, the thermal energy is high which allows for electrons to more easily enter the dot. Thus, low temperatures, decreasing the additional electrons from entering the dot. To read more about the Coulomb blockade, Hanson et al, 2007 does a good job explaining the mechanics behind it.

Animation of Loading and Unloading Dot#

This animation demonstrates the loading and unloading of a quantum dot from the electron wells, and how Fermi energy levels affect the movement (or lack thereof) of the dot. For a single quantum dot, depending on the Fermi energy levels of the source and drain and the M1 gate the electron will either move or stay in place. If the energy of the M1 gate is higher than source, electrons will not be able to move from the source, and a similar blockage occurs when the drain is at a higher Fermi energy than the gate.
